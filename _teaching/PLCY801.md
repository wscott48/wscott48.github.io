PLCY 801 (fall) and PLCY 802 (spring) are our core economics courses that 
provide a foundation for public policy analysis. The primary objectives are to:
(1) introduce the basic principles and techniques of economic theory and (2) 
apply that theory to current public policy issues. PLCY 801 covers 
microeconomics – a focus on the behaviour of individual agents: consumers and 
households, firms, and government, while PLCY 802 covers macroeconomics - 
examining the economy at an aggregate level. Economics builds on core concepts.
It is thus important to have a good understanding of the material each week to 
prepare for subsequent topics. A goal of the course is to provide you with a 
solid foundation in economics that you can apply to policy analysis and build 
upon in other courses in the program. Economics is a factor in many policy 
issues and ‘thinking like an economist’ will be useful in many policy fields. 

The first part of the course in Term 1 covers microeconomic foundations and 
theoretical applications. We begin by introducing the basic model of market 
interaction – supply and demand curves in perfect competition. The first half 
of the term will be spent developing the models of consumer and producer 
behavior underlying these curves. The second half of the term will apply this 
framework to a variety of policy settings and explore what happens when the 
model of perfect competition fails, such as in the presence of monopolies or 
pollution. We will also examine potential policy solutions to correct these 
failures.  


Learning Objectives 
======
Following successful completion of this course, students will be able to:
1.	Understand and contrast models of economic behaviour to analyze the operation of markets, the consequences of market failures, and the rationale for government intervention in the economy,
2.	Evaluate trade-offs involved in public policy formulation using the predictions of economic theory for households, firms, and the government,
3.	Make evidence-based recommendations about the economic aspects of government interventions based on cost-benefit analysis,
4.	Write reasonably complex arguments applying economic theory to contemporaneous policy issues,
5.	Communicate economic theories and their consequences for the public service; and,
6.	Interpret and evaluate the economic aspects of policy reports that one might encounter in the policy world.
